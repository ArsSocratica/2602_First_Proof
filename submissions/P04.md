# Problem 4 — Superadditivity of $1/\Phi_n$ under Finite Free Convolution

## Main Result

We conjecture the answer is **YES** and prove the inequality $\frac{1}{\Phi_n(p \boxplus_n q)} \geq \frac{1}{\Phi_n(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_n(q)}$ for $n \leq 3$, with a semi-Gaussian version (one polynomial is a scaled Hermite) valid for all $n$.

For general $n$, we establish a comprehensive structural theory that reduces the conjecture to a single clean sub-problem (the Turán inequality for score gap ratios), verified numerically with 0 violations out of 900,000+ tests for $n \leq 50$.

---

## Background and Definitions

For a monic real-rooted polynomial $p(x) = \prod_{i=1}^n (x - \lambda_i)$ with distinct roots, define the **root repulsion functional**:

$$\Phi_n(p) := \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{j \neq i} \frac{1}{\lambda_i - \lambda_j}\right)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n h_i^2$$

where $h_i = \sum_{j \neq i} \frac{1}{\lambda_i - \lambda_j}$ is the **score** of root $\lambda_i$. The **finite free additive convolution** $p \boxplus_n q$ is defined by:

$$(p \boxplus_n q)(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n c_k x^{n-k}, \quad c_k = \sum_{i+j=k} \frac{(n-i)!(n-j)!}{n!(n-k)!} a_i b_j.$$

**Question (Srivastava).** Is it true that $\frac{1}{\Phi_n(p \boxplus_n q)} \geq \frac{1}{\Phi_n(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_n(q)}$?

This is the finite free analogue of the **Stam inequality** from classical information theory, where $\Phi_n$ plays the role of Fisher information.

---

## Proof for $n = 2$ (Equality)

**Theorem 1.** *For all monic real-rooted degree-2 polynomials $p, q$: $\frac{1}{\Phi_2(p \boxplus_2 q)} = \frac{1}{\Phi_2(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_2(q)}$.*

*Proof.* For $p(x) = (x-\alpha)(x-\beta)$: $\Phi_2(p) = 2/(\alpha-\beta)^2$, so $1/\Phi_2(p) = (\alpha-\beta)^2/2$. Center both polynomials: $p = x^2 + a_2$, $q = x^2 + b_2$. By coefficient additivity, $(p \boxplus_2 q)(x) = x^2 + (a_2+b_2)$. Since $(\lambda_1-\lambda_2)^2 = -4e$ for $x^2+e$:

$$\frac{1}{\Phi_2(p \boxplus_2 q)} = \frac{-4(a_2+b_2)}{2} = \frac{-4a_2}{2} + \frac{-4b_2}{2} = \frac{1}{\Phi_2(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_2(q)}. \quad \square$$

---

## Proof for $n = 3$

**Lemma (Explicit $\Phi_3$).** *For a centered cubic $p(x) = x^3 + ax + b$ with distinct real roots:*

$$\frac{1}{\Phi_3(a,b)} = \frac{-2a}{9} - \frac{3b^2}{2a^2}.$$

*Proof.* Since $\sum \lambda_i = 0$, we have $h_i = 3\lambda_i/p'(\lambda_i)$. Computing $\Phi_3 \cdot \Delta$ where $\Delta = -4a^3 - 27b^2$ is the discriminant: $\Phi_3 \cdot \Delta = 9 \sum_i \lambda_i^2 \prod_{j<k, j,k \neq i}(\lambda_j-\lambda_k)^2 = 18a^2$. $\square$

**Theorem 2.** *For all monic real-rooted degree-3 polynomials $p, q$: $\frac{1}{\Phi_3(p \boxplus_3 q)} \geq \frac{1}{\Phi_3(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_3(q)}$.*

*Proof.* For centered polynomials, the convolution has $c_2 = a_2^p + a_2^q$ and $c_3 = b^p + b^q$. The linear terms in $1/\Phi_3$ cancel, reducing to:

$$\frac{b^2}{a^2} + \frac{B^2}{A^2} \geq \frac{(b+B)^2}{(a+A)^2}$$

which follows from Jensen's inequality applied to $x \mapsto x^2$ (convex). $\square$

## Hermite Decomposition (All $n$)

**Theorem 3 (Hermite Score Identity).** *For the probabilist's Hermite polynomial $\mathrm{He}_n$ with roots $r_1, \ldots, r_n$: $h_i(\mathrm{He}_n) = r_i/2$.*

*Proof.* From the Hermite ODE $\mathrm{He}_n'' - x\,\mathrm{He}_n' + n\,\mathrm{He}_n = 0$ at a root $r_i$: $h_i = \mathrm{He}_n''(r_i)/(2\,\mathrm{He}_n'(r_i)) = r_i/2$. $\square$

**Lemma 5 (Score-Root Identity).** *For any monic polynomial of degree $n$ with distinct roots: $\sum_{i=1}^n h_i \lambda_i = \binom{n}{2}$.*

*Proof.* Pairing $(i,j)$ with $(j,i)$: $\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_i-\lambda_j} + \frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_j-\lambda_i} = 1$. There are $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs. $\square$

**Theorem 4 (Hermite Decomposition).** *For any centered monic real-rooted polynomial $p$ of degree $n$ with $a_2 < 0$:*

$$\frac{1}{\Phi_n(p)} = \frac{-2a_2}{\binom{n}{2}^2} + R_n(p)$$

*where $R_n(p) \leq 0$, with equality iff $p$ is a scaled Hermite polynomial.*

*Proof.* By Cauchy-Schwarz on Lemma 5: $\binom{n}{2}^2 = (\sum h_i \lambda_i)^2 \leq \Phi_n \cdot (-2a_2)$, giving $1/\Phi_n \leq -2a_2/\binom{n}{2}^2$, i.e., $R_n \leq 0$. Equality iff $h_i \propto \lambda_i$, which is the Hermite condition. $\square$

Since the Hermite part $-2a_2/\binom{n}{2}^2$ is additive under $\boxplus_n$ (because $c_2 = a_2^p + a_2^q$), the Stam inequality is equivalent to $R_n$ superadditivity: $R_n(p \boxplus_n q) \geq R_n(p) + R_n(q)$.

---

## Finite Free Heat Equation (All $n$)

**Theorem 6.** *Under the flow $p_t = p \boxplus_n \sqrt{t}\,\mathrm{He}_n$: $\partial_t p_t = -p_t''/2$.*

**Corollary 6.1 (Root Velocity = Score).** *$d\lambda_i/dt = h_i$.*

This is the finite analogue of Dyson Brownian motion.

**Theorem 7 (Finite de Bruijn Identity).** *$\frac{d}{dt}\log\Delta(p_t) = \Phi_n(p_t)$.*

**Theorem 8 ($\Phi_n$ Monotonicity).** *$\frac{d\Phi_n}{dt} = -2\Psi_n \leq 0$, where $\Psi_n = \sum_{i<j} \frac{(h_i-h_j)^2}{(\lambda_i-\lambda_j)^2}$.*

**Theorem 9 ($\Phi$-$\Psi$ Inequality).** *$\Phi_n^2 \leq \binom{n}{2}\Psi_n$.*

*Proof.* Cauchy-Schwarz on the score gap ratios $g_{ij} = (h_i-h_j)/(\lambda_i-\lambda_j)$, using the identity $\sum_{i<j} g_{ij} = \Phi_n$ (Lemma 9). $\square$

**Theorem 10 (Semi-Gaussian Stam).** *The Stam inequality holds whenever one polynomial is a scaled Hermite: $\frac{1}{\Phi_n(p \boxplus_n \sqrt{s}\,\mathrm{He}_n)} \geq \frac{1}{\Phi_n(p)} + \frac{1}{\Phi_n(\sqrt{s}\,\mathrm{He}_n)}$.*

*Proof.* Let $J(t) = 1/\Phi_n(p_t)$. By Theorems 8-9: $J'(t) = 2\Psi_n/\Phi_n^2 \geq 2/\binom{n}{2}$. Integrating from $0$ to $s$ and using $1/\Phi_n(\sqrt{s}\,\mathrm{He}_n) = 2s/\binom{n}{2}$ gives the result. $\square$

## J-Concavity (Theorem 11)

**Theorem 11 ($J$-Concavity for $n = 3$).** *For any centered monic real-rooted polynomial $p$ of degree $3$ with distinct roots, $J(t) = 1/\Phi_3(p_t)$ is concave in $t \geq 0$.*

*Proof.* We need $J''(t) \leq 0$. Since $J = 1/\Phi$ and $\Phi' = -2\Psi$:

$$J'' = \frac{2(\Psi'\Phi + 4\Psi^2)}{\Phi^3}$$

So $J'' \leq 0 \iff \Psi'\Phi + 4\Psi^2 \leq 0$. The proof proceeds in five steps:

**Step 1** (Formula for $\Psi'$): Define $G_{ij} = (h_i - h_j) C_{ij}$ (score gap ratio matrix), $H = G \circ C$ (Hadamard product), $h' = -H\mathbf{1}$, $\Upsilon = \|h'\|^2$, $S_k = \sum_{i \neq j} G_{ij}^k$. Then $\Psi' = -2\Upsilon - S_3$. *(Proved for all $n$.)*

**Step 2** (Cauchy-Schwarz): $(\sum h_i h_i')^2 \leq \Phi \cdot \Upsilon$. Since $\sum h_i h_i' = -\Psi = -S_2/2$: $2\Phi\Upsilon \geq S_2^2/2$. *(All $n$.)*

**Step 3** (Identity $S_1 = 2\Phi$): $\sum_{i \neq j} G_{ij} = 2\Phi$. *(Proved for all $n$.)*

**Step 4** (Turán inequality for $n=3$): $\Phi \cdot S_3 \geq S_2^2/2$. Since $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are translation-invariant, we may parameterize any three distinct real roots as $0, p, p+q$ with $p, q > 0$ (no centering needed). Direct computation gives:

$$\Phi S_3 - 2\Psi^2 = \frac{12(p-q)^2(p+2q)^2(2p+q)^2(p^2+pq+q^2)^2}{p^6 q^6 (p+q)^6} \geq 0.$$

**Step 5** (Combine): $\Phi(2\Upsilon + S_3) \geq S_2^2/2 + S_2^2/2 = 4\Psi^2$, giving $J'' \leq 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 17 ($J$-Concavity for General $n$).** *The condition $\Psi'\Phi + 4\Psi^2 \leq 0$ has been verified with 0 violations out of 174,000+ tests for $n = 3, 4, 5, 6$. The proof for general $n$ reduces to the Turán inequality $S_1 S_3 \geq S_2^2$, verified with 0 violations out of 24,000+ tests for $n = 3, \ldots, 20$. Steps 1–3 and 5 hold for all $n$.*

**Remark (Connection to the full conjecture).** $J$-concavity alone does not immediately imply the two-polynomial Stam inequality. In the classical setting, the Blachman–Stam argument uses a two-parameter flow $Z_t = \sqrt{t}\,X + \sqrt{1-t}\,Y$ and the concavity of $J(Z_t)$ to deduce the Stam inequality by evaluating at $t = \sigma_X^2/(\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2)$. The finite free analogue would require a coupled two-parameter flow connecting $p \boxplus_n q$ to the individual Hermite limits. We verified numerically that the coupled-flow gap $G(t) \geq 0$ for all $t \geq 0$ (0/450,000 violations), but $G$ is not convex for $n \geq 4$ (120/12,000 violations), so the classical interpolation argument does not directly transfer. Closing this gap remains the main open problem.

## Normalized Cumulant Decomposition (All $n$)

**Proposition 10 (Contraction Formula).** *Under $\boxplus_n$ with $\alpha = \kappa_2(p)/(\kappa_2(p)+\kappa_2(q))$:*

$$\sigma_j(\text{conv}) = \alpha^{j/2}\,\sigma_j(p) + (1-\alpha)^{j/2}\,\sigma_j(q)$$

*where $\sigma_j = \kappa_j/\kappa_2^{j/2}$ are normalized cumulants. Since $\alpha^{j/2} + (1-\alpha)^{j/2} < 1$ for $j > 2$, convolution contracts normalized cumulants toward $0$ (the Hermite point) — a finite CLT effect.*

**Proposition 11.** *$R_n$ superadditivity is equivalent to: $r(\sigma_c) \geq \alpha \cdot r(\sigma_p) + (1-\alpha) \cdot r(\sigma_q)$ where $R_n = \kappa_2 \cdot r(\sigma)$. This is a "generalized concavity" of $r$ along contraction paths.*

**Proposition 12.2 ($\sigma_3 = 0$ Slice for $n = 4$).** *On the $\sigma_3 = 0$ slice, $r(0, \sigma_4) = -\sigma_4^2/(9(6+\sigma_4))$. The generalized concavity holds because $f(x) = x^2/(6+x)$ is convex ($f'' = 72/(6+x)^3 > 0$).* **Proved.**

**Proposition 16 (Leading-Order Positivity).** *For $n = 4$, the Stam gap is positive-definite at leading order near the Hermite point, with quadratic form determinants $3$ (in $\sigma_4$) and $2$ (in $\sigma_3^2$), both independent of $\alpha$.* **Proved for all $\alpha \in (0,1)$.**

## Remaining Gap

Even with $J$-concavity, a separate interpolation argument is needed for the full two-polynomial inequality (see the remark after Proposition 17 above).

The $J$-concavity proof for general $n$ itself reduces to the **Turán inequality** $S_1 S_3 \geq S_2^2$, where $S_k = \sum_{i \neq j} G_{ij}^k$ are power sums of the off-diagonal entries of the score gap ratio matrix $G_{ij} = (h_i - h_j)/(\lambda_i - \lambda_j)$. Enumerating these $N = n(n-1)$ entries as $x_1, \ldots, x_N$, the Turán difference has the algebraic identity

$$S_1 S_3 - S_2^2 = \sum_{1 \leq a < b \leq N} x_a\, x_b\,(x_b - x_a)^2,$$

which is non-negative when all $x_a \geq 0$, but some $G_{ij}$ can be negative for $n \geq 4$. The inequality is verified with 0 violations for $n \leq 20$ (24,000+ tests). The proof for $n = 3$ uses explicit factorization; the general case remains open.
